Current Status

In January 2022, my portfolio in investment account finally reached $300K. It’s an important milestone in my journey to financial independence. I can still remember the moment I opened my TD waterhouse account in 2015. I have grown a lot from a investing newbie to a comfortable investor. I’m not saying I’m already a good investor now and obviously I have made a few mistakes and also had some lucky deals in the past.

Let’s talk about numbers. I have put $216,903 in the last 7 years in my investment account and my current portfolio total value is $322,340 (Feb 10, 2022). By using XIRR function in spreadsheet, my annual return rate is 12%.

I’m the only bread-maker at home till now. We moved from a small house to a bigger one 4 years ago. Thanks to the lowest interest rate ever, our mortgage is only $2K/month. I worked as IT contractor before and recently just converted to permanent employee this year. Frankly speaking, my take-home pay is less than before.

Strategy Review

Like other investors, I used to get ideas of buying stocks from news. When the weed stock came out, I bought some at relative low price and sold them when all media starts to talk about them. That was 1 year before marijuana legalization. Luckily, I made some money from this transaction. Then weed stock went crazy when all media started to report the legalization news. I regretted so much when I saw the stock price went much higher than my sold price. To not miss this great opportunity, I bought the same stock again at a higher price. Unexpectedly, the stock price plunged right after Canada government announced the legalization. It’s so painful to see the price was dropping every day, I decided to sell the stock. This time, the price was only half of my buying price. Ridiculously, the week stock started to pick up again after my sell because the company announced good revenue growth.

I read John Bogle’s book and was persuaded that index ETS is the best way to invest.

For now, I have spend my capital to buy a lot of stocks and some ETFs. If you have a look at my portfolio, many people might think I have too many dividend stocks. However I’m also contributing to a corporate Defined Contribution Pension Plan, which is mainly focusing on Whole World Index. Except this, my wife will have her pension plan. So I think our personal investment will target at generating cash flow. That’s why I think I’ll focus on dividend investing.

Let’s say, I can withdraw $50K from mine and my wife’s pension plan. I think we’ll need extra $30K to live a confortable life which means my anual income will be $80K, the after tax payment should be more than $5000 every month. Note that we assume all income are assumed to be taxable income. If the dividends are from TFSA account, the actual pay will be higher. If we can divide our pay between my wife and me. We should be able to control our income less than $80K.

But anyway, the dividend income from TFSA will be preferable because there is no tax on top of them. So I’ll invest in XEQT in our RRSP account.

For now, our TFSA account have around $80K investment. With annual $12k contribution room and 6% annual growth, the investment will increase to $700K. Using 3% divident rate, we’ll get $21K tax-free income.

So it make sense to me to buy only dividend stock or quality dividend ETFs in my TFSA accounts. On the other hand, I’ll keep invest in our RRSP accounts with the total amount of $130K. With the 6% annual growth, it’s safe to say it will increase to $700K after 20 years.

Adding them together, I’ll sit on around $1.5M portfoloio when I retire 20 years later, which has $40K dividends. With my corporate pension and my wife’s pension, we should be able to live a comfortable life.

For dividends stock, I’ll invest in TSE:XDIV, which has 23 stocks. Among them, I have 8 tickers which stand for 1/3 of the XDIV holdings.
